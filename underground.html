Dinosaurs: Giants of the Ancient World

Have you ever looked down at the ground and wondered what's hiding underneath? You see lots of dirt, rocks, and sand but if you dig a little deeper, you'll find the remains of the incredible beasts that roamed the Earth millions of years ago! Everything we know about Dinosaurs actually comes from what was buried under our feet.

Let's take a step back. What is a Dinosaur?

Dinosaurs are a type of reptile that lived on Earth between 66 and 252 million years ago.

What made them different than other reptiles is that their legs were directly under their bodies, not sticking out to the sides like other reptiles you might know (a lizard). That evolutionary trait is what helped them walk and run as quickly as they did!

Mythbusters!

Here are some animals that people commonly think are dinosaurs, but actually aren't

  • Pterodactyls (the flying ones) were close cousins but belong to a separate group called pterosaurs.
  • Plesiosaurs and Mosasaurs (the ocean reptiles) were their own thing too, not true dinosaurs.
  • Woolly mammoths aren't dinosaurs at all! They were mammals and lived way more recently.

The Three Periods of the Dinosaur Age

Dinosaurs didn't all exist at the same time. They lived across three different time periods!

  • Triassic Period (252–201 million years ago): This is when the first Dinosaur fossils are dated from! They were much smaller than the Dinosaurs you probably think of.
  • Jurassic Period (201–145 million years ago): This is when Dinosaurs were able to take over the planet! Most common are giant reptiles like Braciosaurus and Stegosaurus.
  • Cretaceous Period (145–66 million years ago): The most diverse Dinosaurs exist during this time period: from T. Rex, to Triceratops. But this is also, sadly, the phase when dinosaurs went extinct.

Herbivores vs. Carnivores vs. Omnivores

Just like animals today, dinosaurs ate very different things! Herbivores only ate plants, carnivores only ate meat, and omnivores ate both. Can you guess which category T. rex falls into?

How Do We Know About Dinosaurs? Fossils!

No human has ever seen a real live dinosaur -- so how do scientists know so much about them? The answer is fossils! A fossil forms when an animal dies and gets buried under layers of mud or sand. Over millions of years, the bones slowly turn to rock. Eventually, they make their way back to the surface where a lucky scientist might spot them!

Types of Fossils

  • Body fossils are actual preserved remains like bones, teeth, shells, and claws. These are what you see in museums!
  • Trace fossils are clues left behind by behavior, like footprints, burrows, or even fossilized poop (scientists call that a coprolite -- yuck!).
  • Mold and cast fossils: These form when a bone dissolves underground but leaves behind a perfect hollow shape. That shape later fills in with minerals, creating a rock copy of the original bone!

Where And How Can I Find Fossils?

Scientists, known as Paleontologists, have found fossils on every continent! That includes deserts in Mongolia, Patagonia in Argentina, and even Antarctica!

I want to meet Dinosaurs. Are They Actually Extinct?

Actually, birds are living dinosaurs! Birds evolved from a group of small, feathered dinosaurs called theropods during the Jurassic Period. So the next time you see a pigeon, you're basically looking at a tiny dinosaur! So, what came first the chicken or the egg?

Scientists found fossils of dinosaurs that had feathers, just like birds do today! Over millions of years, those feathered dinosaurs slowly evolved into the birds we see flying around outside. So technically, every time you see a bird, you're looking at a living dinosaur!

What Happened to the Non-Avian Dinosaurs then? The ones that can't fly?

So what happened to all the other dinosaurs, the ones that couldn't fly? About 66 million years ago, a massive asteroid crashed into Earth near what is now the Yucatán Peninsula in Mexico. The impact sent a enormous cloud of dust and debris into the atmosphere, blocking out sunlight for months. Without sunlight, plants could not survive. Without plants, herbivores had nothing to eat. And without herbivores, carnivores had nothing to eat either. This chain reaction wiped out almost 75% of all life on Earth, including all non-avian dinosaurs. Scientists call this the Cretaceous-Paleogene extinction event. The small, feathered dinosaurs that were ancestors of modern birds managed to survive, which is why we still have birds today!
